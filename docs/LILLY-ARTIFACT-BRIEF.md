# Lilly Artifact v2 — Working Brief
Started: 2026-04-03
Status: In development

*This is a working document for the next artifact to share with Laree Ross and the Lilly innovation team. Separate from the Dispatch mandate — this is engagement-specific.*

---

## WHAT v1 GOT WRONG

The first artifact (lilly.goodliving.studio) was a strategy deck about Lilly's business. Laree's feedback was direct: it reads as if you're assuming they don't already know this. The framing was backward — it demonstrated research, not capability.

## WHAT v2 NEEDS TO BE

The artifact should answer one question: **what becomes possible when someone who thinks like this operates inside your organization?**

It should lead with Jeremy — his capabilities, his tools, his way of working — and let the Lilly relevance emerge from the demonstration rather than the narration.

---

## POSSIBLE DIRECTIONS

### Direction A: "The Intelligence System"
Show Dispatch itself — not as a portfolio piece, but as evidence of how Jeremy thinks about information architecture, AI direction, and decision-support systems. Frame it as: "This is how I process the landscape. Imagine this capability pointed inward at Lilly's innovation challenges."

*Strength:* Authentic. It's real, working software. Demonstrates AI-direction skill directly.
*Risk:* Could feel self-referential. Needs to bridge to Lilly-specific value clearly.

### Direction B: "The Prototype"
Build a working prototype of something Lilly could actually use — a patient experience concept, a care coordination tool, a clinical trial communication system. Demonstrate the design-to-working-software pipeline with AI-augmented execution.

*Strength:* Tangible. Shows what the capability produces, not just what it is.
*Risk:* Needs to be good enough that it doesn't look naive about healthcare complexity. Can't be a toy.

### Direction C: "The Case Study as Prototype"
A hybrid: take a real Lilly challenge (LillyDirect patient experience, Donanemab care coordination, Alzheimer's diagnostic pathway) and build a designed, interactive artifact that demonstrates how Jeremy would approach it — not as a strategy deck but as an explorable prototype with real data, real interactions, and a clear design point of view.

*Strength:* Best of both. Shows thinking AND execution. The artifact itself is the evidence.
*Risk:* Scope. Needs to be scoped tightly enough to be excellent, not comprehensive.

---

## WHAT THE ARTIFACT SHOULD DEMONSTRATE

Regardless of direction, the artifact must evidence these capabilities:

1. **AI direction** — Jeremy didn't hand-code every pixel. He directed AI agents to build working software. The artifact should make visible the fact that this is how he works — design leadership at the system level, not the component level.

2. **Strategic framing** — The artifact should contain a point of view, not just a deliverable. What does Jeremy see about Lilly's situation that they might not be seeing themselves? Not "here's your business" but "here's what I notice from the outside that might be invisible from the inside."

3. **Design authority** — The artifact should be beautiful. Not decoratively beautiful — structurally beautiful. The information architecture, the typography, the interaction model should all feel like they were designed by someone with real craft authority.

4. **Technical fluency** — The artifact should be a working web application, not a PDF or a Figma prototype. It should load fast, work on mobile, and feel like production software. This demonstrates the "design leader who can ship" thesis.
